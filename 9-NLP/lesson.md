# Lesson: Natural Language Processing

---

## Objective

Understand how to extract meaning from text.

---

## Why This Matters

A large amount of useful data is stored as text.

For example:

* student feedback
* survey responses
* emails and messages

Structured data tells us *what happened*.

Text helps us understand:

> **why it happened**

---

## The Common Mistake

Many learners try to:

* jump straight into models

But NLP starts with:

* cleaning
* understanding
* interpreting

Without these steps, results can be misleading.

---

## What is NLP?

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is used to:

* analyse text
* extract meaning
* identify patterns

It answers questions like:

> “What are people saying, and what does it mean?”

---

## Worked Example

Consider this student comment:

> “The course is interesting but the workload is too heavy.”

---

### Data

Raw text comment

---

### Information

* positive signal: “interesting”
* negative signal: “too heavy”

---

### Interpretation

The student finds the course engaging but challenging in terms of workload

---

### Insight

There may be a need to review workload balance

---

This shows how NLP helps move from:

**text → meaning → insight → action**

---

## Key Steps

A simple NLP workflow:

1. Clean text (remove noise, standardise format)
2. Tokenise (split into words)
3. Count or analyse words
4. Interpret results

These steps help convert unstructured text into usable information.

---

## Common Tasks

Typical NLP tasks include:

* word frequency → what is mentioned often
* sentiment analysis → positive or negative tone
* topic identification → what themes exist

---

## When to Use This

Use NLP when:

* data is in text form
* you want to understand experiences or opinions
* you need to identify themes or issues

Examples:

* student feedback analysis
* customer reviews
* survey comments

A simple rule:

> If the data is text, you need NLP to make sense of it.

---

## Common Pitfall

A common mistake is to over-simplify text.

For example:

* treating sentiment as purely positive or negative
* ignoring context or nuance
* assuming word counts fully represent meaning

Text is messy and subjective.

Always interpret results carefully.

---

## Why This Matters

Text data helps us:

* understand experiences
* identify pain points
* improve decisions
