# Exercise Solutions: Prescriptive Analytics

---

## 1. Selecting 3 Students to Prioritise

A simple approach is to select the students with the highest risk scores:

* A
* B
* C

This is a reasonable starting point because these students appear most at risk.

---

## 2. Why This Choice Makes Sense

This decision assumes:

* higher predicted risk means higher priority
* resources should be focused on the most at-risk students

This is often the most intuitive rule when no other information is available.

---

## 3. What This Approach Ignores

Although this approach is simple, it may ignore:

* the cost of intervention
* whether some students are easier to help
* whether limited resources could be used more efficiently

This means the “highest risk first” rule may not always give the best overall result.

---

## 4. If Resources Increase

If more resources become available, the decision may change.

For example:

* more students can be included
* selection can extend beyond the top 3
* there may be more flexibility to support both high-risk and moderate-risk students

This shows that prescriptive decisions depend on constraints.

---

## Reflection

A strong response should recognise that:

* prediction provides information
* decision-making requires prioritisation
* constraints influence what is possible

---

## Key Note

There is no single perfect rule.

A good response should:

* explain the selection clearly
* recognise trade-offs
* connect prediction to action
