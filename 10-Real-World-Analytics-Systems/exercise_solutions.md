# Solution and Discussion: Designing an Analytics System

---

## Case 1: Education

### Data

* student logins
* assignment submission
* forum activity

---

### Analysis

* identify low engagement patterns
* possible use of classification model to predict risk

---

### Decision

* which students should be prioritised for intervention

---

### Action

* outreach to low-engagement students
* academic or advisory support

---

### Risk / Limitation

* false positives (students flagged but not actually at risk)
* over-intervention leading to fatigue

---

## Case 2: E-commerce

### Data

* purchase history
* browsing behaviour
* time since last visit
* support interactions

---

### Analysis

* churn prediction
* segmentation of customer behaviour

---

### Decision

* which customers are likely to churn

---

### Action

* targeted promotions
* re-engagement campaigns

---

### Risk / Limitation

* unnecessary discounts
* reduced profit margins

---

## Case 3: Healthcare

### Data

* patient demographics
* visit history
* risk scores

---

### Analysis

* risk scoring
* prioritisation models

---

### Decision

* which patients require urgent attention

---

### Action

* prioritise treatment or monitoring

---

### Risk / Limitation

* misclassification risk
* ethical implications of incorrect prioritisation

---

## Discussion

Across all three cases, the structure is the same:

```text id="a0czp9"
Data → Analysis → Decision → Action
```

---

### Key Insight 1

Different domains use different data and techniques.

But:

> **the decision structure remains consistent**

---

### Key Insight 2

Analytics does not end at analysis.

> **value is created only when decisions lead to action**

---

### Key Insight 3

Every system has risks.

Good analytics design includes:

* awareness of limitations
* consideration of trade-offs

---

## Final Takeaway

You are not solving three problems.

> **You are applying one system across different contexts.**
