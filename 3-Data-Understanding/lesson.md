# Lesson: Data Understanding

---

## Objective

Learn how to explore and understand a dataset before analysis.

---

## Why This Matters

Good analysis starts with good understanding of the data.

If you misunderstand the data:

* patterns may be misinterpreted
* conclusions may be incorrect
* decisions may be flawed

Even simple mistakes in understanding can lead to wrong insights.

---

## The Common Mistake

Many people jump straight into modelling.

They focus on:

* building models
* running algorithms

But without understanding the data:

* patterns are misinterpreted
* models become unreliable

A model cannot fix poor understanding of data.

---

## What is Data Understanding?

Data understanding involves:

* exploring structure
* summarising variables
* identifying patterns
* checking data quality

It is about answering:

> **What is in this dataset, and what does it mean?**

---

## Key Questions

When you see a dataset, ask:

* What does each column represent?
* What type of data is it?
* Are there missing values?
* Are there unusual or extreme values?
* Does the data make sense in context?

These questions help you avoid incorrect assumptions.

---

## Types of Data

Understanding data types helps determine how you analyse it.

* **Numerical** (e.g. number of logins)
* **Categorical** (e.g. submitted: yes/no)

Different types of data require different approaches.

---

## Descriptive Statistics

Summaries help you understand:

* average (central tendency)
* spread (variation)
* distribution (shape of data)

These provide a quick overview of the dataset before deeper analysis.

---

## Worked Example

Consider a student with:

* 0 logins
* no assignment submission

### Data

0 logins, no submission

---

### Information

The student shows low engagement

---

### Interpretation

The student may be disengaged or inactive

---

This shows how simple data points can already provide useful signals.

---

## When to Use This

Data understanding should be done:

* at the start of every analysis
* before building any model
* when working with a new dataset
* when results seem unexpected

A simple rule:

> If you don’t understand your data, you should not analyse it.

---

## Common Pitfall

A common mistake is to rely on tools without thinking.

For example:

* running models without checking data quality
* ignoring missing or incorrect values
* assuming the data is correct

This can lead to:

* misleading insights
* incorrect conclusions

Always question the data before trusting it.
