## 2. Comparing Treatment vs Control

The treatment group has a higher improvement rate than the control group:

* Treatment → 75%
* Control → 25%

This suggests that the intervention may have helped.

---

## 3. Did the Intervention Work?

A reasonable first interpretation is:

* the intervention appears to be associated with better outcomes

Because the treatment group improved more than the control group, there is **some evidence** that the intervention made a difference.

However, this conclusion depends on whether the comparison is fair.

---

## 4. Is This Comparison Enough to Claim Causation?

Not necessarily.

To claim causation confidently, we would need to know that:

* the treatment and control groups were comparable before the intervention
* the intervention was the main meaningful difference between them

If the groups were not similar at baseline, then the observed difference might be explained by other factors.

---

## Reflection

### Is the difference meaningful?

Yes, the difference is meaningful in a practical sense because the improvement rates are quite different.

However, meaningful difference in outcomes is not the same as proof of causation.

---

### What assumptions are you making?

A strong answer should recognise assumptions such as:

* the groups were similar before the intervention
* no major outside factor affected one group differently
* improvement was measured consistently for both groups

---

### What would have happened if the intervention was not implemented?

This is the key causal question.

We cannot directly observe it.

For the treatment group, we do not know what would have happened without intervention.

This is why causal thinking is difficult.

---

### What additional data would make the conclusion more reliable?

Examples include:

* baseline performance or engagement
* whether assignment to treatment was random
* before-and-after measurements
* information about other factors affecting students

These would help us judge whether the treatment and control groups were fairly comparable.

---

## Key Note

A good answer should not simply say:

> “Treatment did better, so the intervention worked.”

A stronger answer says:

* the treatment group improved more
* this suggests the intervention may have worked
* but causal interpretation depends on whether the comparison is fair
