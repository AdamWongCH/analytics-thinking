# Lesson: Problem Framing

---

## Objective

Learn how to move from a vague request to a clear problem that supports a decision.

---

## Start with the Request

A stakeholder says:

> “We need a dashboard to monitor engagement.”

This sounds reasonable.

But it is not yet a useful starting point for analysis.

---

## Why This Is Not Enough

A request like this tells you:

* what to build

But it does not tell you:

* what problem you are solving
* what decision needs to be made
* what action will follow

If you start from the request, you risk building something that looks useful but does not lead to action.

---

## What a Strong Analyst Does

Instead of jumping into data, a strong analyst pauses and asks:

* What is the real problem behind this request?
* What decision are we trying to support?

---

## From Request to Problem

Let’s transform the request step by step.

---

### Step 1: Clarify the intent

What might “monitor engagement” really mean?

Possible intent:

* detect students who are disengaging
* identify students who may drop out

---

### Step 2: Define the problem

A clearer problem statement could be:

> Identify students with low engagement in the first 4 weeks

Now we have something specific and actionable.

---

### Step 3: Define the decision

Once the problem is clear, the decision becomes clearer:

> Which students should we prioritise for intervention?

---

## Why This Matters

Two analysts can receive the same request:

> “Build a dashboard”

But produce very different outcomes:

* one builds charts
* the other enables action

The difference is not technical skill.

It is problem framing.

---

## A Simple Check

Before working with data, ask:

* Can I clearly state the problem?
* Can I clearly state the decision?

If not, you are not ready to analyse.

---

## Key Takeaway

Do not start with the request.

> **Start by defining the problem that leads to a decision.**
