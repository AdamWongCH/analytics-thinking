# Lesson: Problem Framing

---

## Objective

Learn how to define the right problem before working with data.

---

## The Common Mistake

A stakeholder says:

“Build me a dashboard.”

Most analysts:

* start collecting data
* start building visuals

But pause for a moment.

What problem are we solving?

---

## Request vs Problem

A request is not a problem.

| Request           | Problem                                |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Build a dashboard | Identify at-risk students early        |
| Track engagement  | Improve retention through intervention |

---

## What is a Well-Defined Problem?

A good problem includes:

* A clear objective
* A decision to be made
* An outcome that matters

---

## Example

Request:
“Monitor student engagement”

Better problem:
“Identify students with low engagement in the first 4 weeks so that early intervention can be triggered.”

---

## Key Questions to Ask

* What decision will this support?
* Who is making the decision?
* What action will be taken?
* What does success look like?

---

## Defining Success

If you cannot define success, you cannot evaluate your work.

Examples:

* reduce dropout rate
* increase engagement
* improve completion rates

---

## From Problem to Question

A well-framed problem leads to a clear analytical question:

“Which students show early signs of disengagement?”

---

## How this connects

In the exercise, you will:

* refine vague requests into clear problems

In the lab, you will:

* structure a problem using a simple framework

---

## Key Takeaway

Better problem framing leads to better analytics outcomes.
